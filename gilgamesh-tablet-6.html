Tablet VI: Ishtar and the Bull of Heaven

Gilgamesh rejects Ishtar’s marriage proposal, provoking the release of the Bull of Heaven and bringing the heroes into more direct conflict with divine authority.

Tablet VI at a Glance

After defeating Humbaba and returning from the Forest of Cedar, Gilgamesh washes, changes his clothing, and displays his royal appearance. The goddess Ishtar sees him and proposes marriage, promising wealth, political power, fertility, and public honour.

Gilgamesh refuses. Rather than declining carefully, he insults Ishtar and lists the destructive fates suffered by her former lovers. Ishtar responds by asking her father Anu for the Bull of Heaven so that she can punish Gilgamesh and attack Uruk.

The Bull causes death and destruction in the city, but Gilgamesh and Enkidu work together to kill it. Their victory strengthens their heroic reputation, yet Enkidu then insults Ishtar directly by throwing part of the Bull toward her.

Tablet VI therefore appears victorious on the surface, but it prepares the tragedy of the following tablets. Gilgamesh and Enkidu have now killed both Humbaba and the Bull of Heaven, challenged divine authority, and publicly humiliated a powerful goddess.

Detailed Sequence of Events

1. Gilgamesh returns from the Cedar Forest

After killing Humbaba and cutting the cedars, Gilgamesh returns from the expedition with the fame-producing achievement he had desired.

He is no longer simply the oppressive king introduced at the beginning of the epic. He now appears as a successful hero who has travelled beyond Uruk, defeated a terrifying guardian, and returned with valuable timber.

However, the moral consequences of the expedition have not disappeared. Humbaba’s curse and the destruction of the sacred forest remain unresolved.

2. Gilgamesh washes and dresses himself

Gilgamesh cleans his weapons and body, arranges his hair, and puts on royal clothing. These actions restore him from the disorder of the wilderness to the appearance of a king within the city.

The scene emphasizes his beauty and public status. His transformation in appearance is what attracts Ishtar’s attention.

Clothing again marks movement between worlds. Enkidu previously received clothing when entering civilization; Gilgamesh now changes from warrior-traveller back into visible royal authority.

3. Ishtar sees Gilgamesh

Ishtar is attracted to Gilgamesh’s appearance and invites him to become her husband.

Her proposal is not an ordinary private marriage request. Ishtar is a powerful goddess, so marriage to her would connect Gilgamesh’s kingship with divine power.

The proposal also introduces a new type of danger. Gilgamesh has survived physical threats, but he must now respond to an offer from a deity whose power greatly exceeds his own.

4. Ishtar promises wealth and royal success

Ishtar offers Gilgamesh an impressive marriage settlement. She promises luxurious vehicles, precious materials, powerful rulers bowing before him, fertile animals, and prosperity.

Her offer appeals directly to royal ambition. The promised gifts would increase Gilgamesh’s wealth, prestige, military power, and public glory.

The proposal therefore tests whether Gilgamesh can be persuaded by the same desire for greatness that motivated the Cedar Forest expedition.

5. Gilgamesh questions what marriage to Ishtar would cost

Gilgamesh does not accept the proposal. He questions how he could provide for a goddess and what kind of relationship she would offer him in return.

His response quickly becomes hostile. He compares Ishtar to objects that fail when they are needed, such as defective structures, unreliable equipment, or things that cause discomfort rather than protection.

These comparisons accuse her of being unstable, dangerous, and incapable of loyalty.

6. Gilgamesh lists Ishtar’s former lovers

Gilgamesh strengthens his rejection by recounting what happened to several of Ishtar’s previous lovers.

The list includes divine, human, and animal figures. Each relationship ends in suffering, transformation, captivity, violence, or loss.

Gilgamesh’s argument is that Ishtar’s love is temporary and destructive. He believes that accepting her would eventually make him another victim.

7. The story of Dumuzi

Gilgamesh refers to Dumuzi, also known as Tammuz, a figure closely connected with Ishtar in Mesopotamian tradition.

Dumuzi’s fate is associated with lamentation, loss, and the underworld. Gilgamesh uses him as evidence that even a privileged divine lover did not remain safe.

The reference would have carried religious and mythological meaning for an ancient audience familiar with rituals of mourning for Dumuzi.

8. The transformed lovers

Gilgamesh continues by recalling lovers whom Ishtar transformed or punished after losing interest in them.

Depending on the figure, the punishment involves becoming an animal, being trapped, being beaten, or becoming rejected by those who once depended on him.

The list creates a pattern: Ishtar desires a figure, gains power over him, and then leaves him damaged.

9. Gilgamesh’s rejection becomes a public insult

Gilgamesh could have rejected Ishtar without attacking her character. Instead, he humiliates her by presenting her entire history as proof that she is untrustworthy.

His answer may be intelligent in recognizing danger, but it is also politically reckless. He speaks to a powerful goddess as though she were an ordinary opponent he can defeat through insults.

This is another example of Gilgamesh failing to distinguish courage from provocation.

10. Ishtar goes to Anu and Antu

Humiliated, Ishtar goes to the divine world and complains to her father Anu and her mother Antu.

She presents Gilgamesh’s response as an intolerable insult against her dignity.

The conflict therefore moves beyond Gilgamesh and Ishtar as individuals. It becomes a dispute involving the divine hierarchy and the punishment of a human king.

11. Anu questions Ishtar

Anu does not immediately agree to her demand. He points out that Gilgamesh’s insults were based on Ishtar’s own past actions.

This response suggests that the gods do not automatically support one another. Anu can recognize that Ishtar helped create the situation.

However, his criticism does not prevent him from eventually giving her the weapon she requests.

12. Ishtar demands the Bull of Heaven

Ishtar asks Anu to give her the Bull of Heaven so she can punish Gilgamesh and attack Uruk.

She threatens severe consequences if he refuses. Her demand shows that the conflict has shifted from wounded pride to large-scale destructive retaliation.

The Bull is not simply an animal. It is a supernatural force whose release threatens the whole human community.

13. Anu warns about famine

Anu warns that releasing the Bull will create years of scarcity and threaten the food supply.

Ishtar responds that she has already prepared stored grain and supplies for the population and animals.

This exchange emphasizes that divine conflict has agricultural and social consequences. The punishment of one king can endanger an entire city.

14. Anu gives Ishtar the Bull

Anu finally hands over the Bull of Heaven, and Ishtar brings it down against Uruk.

Anu’s agreement does not necessarily mean that Gilgamesh’s punishment is morally justified. It demonstrates how divine power can be used through personal loyalties, pressure, and competing interests.

The episode again presents the divine world as powerful but not governed by one consistent moral judgment.

15. The Bull attacks Uruk

The Bull creates destruction through its movements and breath. The ground opens, large numbers of men fall, and the city is thrown into danger.

Gilgamesh’s personal confrontation with Ishtar now harms people who had no role in insulting her.

The scene raises a political question: when a ruler provokes a more powerful force, who suffers the consequences?

16. Enkidu confronts the Bull

Enkidu positions himself against the Bull and studies how it moves. His role is practical and physical: he must restrain or expose it so that Gilgamesh can strike.

Their method resembles cooperation in hunting or combat. One companion controls the enemy while the other delivers the decisive blow.

Their success depends once again on coordinated friendship rather than isolated heroism.

17. Gilgamesh kills the Bull

Enkidu seizes or holds the Bull while Gilgamesh attacks its vulnerable point and kills it.

The scene confirms that the two heroes have become an exceptionally effective pair.

However, this victory is even more dangerous than killing Humbaba. The Bull has been directly sent by a goddess and supplied by Anu.

18. The heroes offer the Bull’s heart

After killing the Bull, Gilgamesh and Enkidu remove its heart and present it to Shamash.

The offering recognizes Shamash as their divine protector and places the victory within a religious framework.

Yet offering part of the Bull to one god does not erase the offence against the divine figures connected with its release.

19. Ishtar mourns and curses Gilgamesh

Ishtar stands on the city wall and laments the Bull’s death while cursing Gilgamesh.

Her position on the wall mirrors the public nature of the conflict. The struggle between goddess and king is now witnessed by the city.

Her grief and anger show that the death of the Bull has not ended the confrontation.

20. Enkidu insults Ishtar

Enkidu tears off part of the Bull and throws it toward Ishtar, declaring that he would treat her similarly if he could reach her.

This action is more than celebration. It is a direct physical and verbal humiliation of a goddess.

Enkidu’s behaviour turns victory into sacrilege. His aggression helps explain why divine punishment becomes unavoidable.

21. Ishtar gathers mourners

Ishtar gathers women associated with her worship to mourn over the Bull.

The mourning gives the Bull’s death ritual and emotional importance. It is not treated merely as the destruction of a monster.

The scene also contrasts with the heroes’ celebration. What appears as triumph in one community appears as death and loss in another.

22. Gilgamesh celebrates the victory

Gilgamesh displays the Bull’s horns, dedicates objects, and celebrates with Enkidu and the people of Uruk.

The victory increases his public reputation. He has now defeated two supernatural opponents in succession.

But the celebration is filled with dramatic irony. The audience has already seen how seriously the heroes have offended the gods.

23. Gilgamesh asks who is the greatest hero

Gilgamesh publicly asks who is the most magnificent or heroic among men, and the crowd responds by praising him and Enkidu.

The exchange shows the height of their fame and confidence.

It also marks the point at which heroic self-celebration becomes closest to arrogance. Their success has made them feel nearly untouchable.

24. Enkidu has a disturbing dream

The tablet ends by moving away from celebration. Enkidu experiences a dream that prepares for the gods’ judgment.

The dream changes the emotional direction of the epic. Triumph is immediately followed by the suggestion of punishment and death.

The consequences of killing Humbaba and the Bull of Heaven will become the central subject of Tablet VII.

Difficult Ideas Explained

Why does Ishtar want to marry Gilgamesh?

Gilgamesh has returned as a beautiful, powerful, and successful king. Ishtar’s attraction may be personal, but marriage would also create a connection between divine authority and royal power.

Why does Gilgamesh reject her?

He believes her history shows that she destroys or abandons those she loves. His rejection is based on self-protection rather than simple dislike.

The greater mistake is not necessarily refusing her. It is refusing her through a long and humiliating public attack.

Are Gilgamesh’s accusations true?

The stories he lists belong to wider Mesopotamian mythology, but his speech is also designed to insult Ishtar. He selects examples that support his argument and presents her relationships in the most damaging way possible.

Why does Anu initially criticize Ishtar?

Anu recognizes that Gilgamesh’s insults refer to Ishtar’s own past behaviour. This shows that divine family loyalty does not eliminate disagreement.

Why does Anu still give her the Bull?

Ishtar pressures and threatens him while claiming that she has prepared for the resulting famine. Anu ultimately gives in despite recognizing the destructive consequences.

What does the Bull of Heaven represent?

Within the story, it is a supernatural divine weapon capable of attacking the city and damaging the land.

It also represents the scale of divine retaliation: one insult produces consequences for an entire human population.

Why does the Bull create cracks in the earth?

The Bull’s force is supernatural rather than zoologically realistic. Its breath and movement disturb the ground itself, emphasizing that the heroes face a force of cosmic or divine destruction.

Why does Enkidu throw part of the Bull at Ishtar?

Enkidu is celebrating victory and expressing contempt for the goddess who sent the Bull.

However, his action crosses from resistance into deliberate humiliation. It becomes one of the clearest reasons for divine punishment.

Why do the heroes offer the heart to Shamash?

Shamash has repeatedly protected them, so the offering acknowledges him as their divine supporter.

It also reveals divisions among the gods: the heroes honour Shamash while openly opposing Ishtar and the divine forces behind the Bull.

Why does the tablet end with a dream?

The dream interrupts the celebration and warns that heroic success has not protected the companions from judgment.

Structurally, it turns the epic away from adventure and toward loss, grief, and mortality.

Major Themes in Tablet VI

Pride and provocation

Gilgamesh may be justified in refusing Ishtar, but the form of his refusal turns caution into dangerous humiliation.

Sexuality and power

Ishtar’s proposal combines desire, marriage, political authority, wealth, and divine power. The episode does not separate personal attraction from public status.

The instability of divine authority

Anu questions Ishtar but ultimately supports her retaliation. The gods possess enormous power without always exercising unified or restrained judgment.

Private conflict and public suffering

Ishtar’s anger is directed toward Gilgamesh, but the Bull kills and threatens ordinary people in Uruk.

Friendship as coordinated strength

Gilgamesh and Enkidu defeat the Bull because they divide the task and act together.

Friendship as mutual recklessness

Their bond also encourages escalation. Rather than restraining each other after victory, they celebrate and insult Ishtar further.

Heroism and sacrilege

Killing the Bull saves Uruk, but the heroes are also destroying a divine creature and openly humiliating a goddess.

The danger of excessive success

The heroes have survived every challenge so far. Their repeated victories create the illusion that strength can overcome all limits, including divine ones.

Celebration before tragedy

The public celebration represents the highest point of the heroes’ shared fame. The dream at the end shows that this peak will be followed immediately by collapse.

Why Tablet VI Matters

Tablet VI is the final great victory of Gilgamesh and Enkidu as a heroic pair. Their cooperation appears almost unstoppable: they have defeated Humbaba, survived the forest, and now killed the Bull of Heaven.

Yet the tablet also exposes the danger of their success. Gilgamesh turns a defensible rejection into an insult, Ishtar turns personal humiliation into mass destruction, and Enkidu turns victory into a direct act of contempt against a goddess.

Most importantly, the tablet completes the chain of offences that leads to Enkidu’s death. The epic now changes direction. The first half has concentrated on friendship, adventure, fame, and conquest; the second half will concentrate on grief, mortality, and the failure to escape human limits.

Questions to Consider

  • Is Gilgamesh right to reject Ishtar?
  • At what point does his rejection become unnecessarily dangerous?
  • Does Ishtar want Gilgamesh personally, politically, or both?
  • Why does Anu release the Bull after recognizing that Ishtar helped provoke the conflict?
  • Who is responsible for the deaths caused by the Bull?
  • Does killing the Bull make Gilgamesh and Enkidu defenders of Uruk or offenders against the gods?
  • Why is Enkidu’s insult more serious than merely defeating the Bull?
  • How does friendship help the heroes succeed?
  • How does friendship also make them more reckless?
  • Why does the epic place its greatest celebration immediately before the beginning of tragedy?
