Tablet VII: The Death of Enkidu

The gods judge the heroes’ actions, Enkidu struggles against his fate, and the epic turns from heroic adventure toward grief and mortality.

Tablet VII at a Glance

Tablet VII begins after Gilgamesh and Enkidu have killed Humbaba and the Bull of Heaven. Enkidu dreams that the gods gather to judge their actions. Although both men participated in the killings, the gods decide that Enkidu must die.

As Enkidu becomes ill, he responds with anger. He curses the great cedar door he helped construct, the hunter who first discovered him, and Shamhat, whose actions brought him from the wilderness into civilization.

Shamash speaks to Enkidu and reminds him of everything he gained through Shamhat: food, clothing, honour, friendship, and the love of Gilgamesh. Enkidu withdraws his curse and blesses her instead.

Enkidu then dreams of a terrifying journey to the underworld, where human distinctions of rank and power disappear. His illness worsens, and after several days of suffering he dies.

The tablet transforms the epic. Until this point, death has been something Gilgamesh discusses abstractly while pursuing fame. Enkidu’s death makes mortality immediate, personal, and emotionally unbearable.

Detailed Sequence of Events

1. Enkidu dreams of the divine council

Enkidu tells Gilgamesh that he has seen the gods gathered in council. Anu, Enlil, Ea, and Shamash discuss the killings of Humbaba and the Bull of Heaven.

The dream reveals that the heroes’ victories have not simply ended with celebration. Their actions have entered the realm of divine judgment.

The council also shows that heroic success does not place Gilgamesh and Enkidu beyond accountability. Their physical power cannot protect them from decisions made by the gods.

2. The gods review the heroes’ offences

The gods consider the killing of Humbaba, the guardian of the Cedar Forest, and the killing of the Bull of Heaven.

These events were celebrated in Uruk as heroic victories, but from the divine perspective they represent violations of sacred authority.

Tablet VII therefore forces the reader to reconsider earlier events. A deed can appear glorious to humans while appearing criminal to the gods.

3. Enlil declares that one hero must die

The divine council concludes that the actions of Gilgamesh and Enkidu require a death. Enkidu is selected as the one who must pay.

The judgment is painful because both companions participated in the offences. Gilgamesh proposed the Cedar Forest expedition and struck Humbaba, while both men killed the Bull.

The decision therefore appears unequal from a human perspective. The epic does not offer a simple explanation for why Enkidu rather than Gilgamesh is chosen.

4. Shamash attempts to defend the heroes

Shamash has repeatedly supported Gilgamesh and Enkidu. He helped them during the journey and sent the winds that trapped Humbaba.

His involvement makes the divine judgment complicated. The heroes did not act without divine assistance, yet they are now punished for the resulting actions.

This reflects the epic’s presentation of the gods as divided rather than perfectly unified. Divine support does not guarantee permanent protection.

5. Enkidu understands that he will die

Enkidu recognizes the dream as a true announcement of his fate. The threat is no longer an enemy that can be fought physically.

Humbaba and the Bull could be attacked with weapons and teamwork. A divine death sentence cannot be defeated in the same way.

Enkidu’s helplessness introduces a new kind of conflict: the human struggle against an unavoidable limit.

6. Enkidu becomes ill

Enkidu is struck by a serious illness and becomes confined to bed. His powerful body, which once matched Gilgamesh and defeated supernatural opponents, begins to fail.

The contrast is deliberate. A hero capable of extraordinary combat can still be destroyed by sickness.

Illness removes Enkidu’s independence and forces him to wait for a death he cannot prevent.

7. Enkidu curses the cedar door

Enkidu turns his anger toward the enormous cedar door made from the trees cut in Humbaba’s forest.

The door represents the heroes’ achievement and the transformation of the sacred forest into a human monument. Enkidu now sees that achievement as connected to his punishment.

He regrets the labour and destruction that produced it and wishes he had damaged or abandoned it rather than helping create it.

8. Why Enkidu attacks an object

The door cannot respond, but it gives Enkidu something visible to blame. His true enemy is fate, which cannot be physically confronted.

By cursing the door, Enkidu directs his anger toward a symbol of the expedition that brought him glory and death.

The episode shows how grief and fear often search for a concrete target even when the real cause cannot be reached.

9. Gilgamesh hears Enkidu’s despair

Gilgamesh remains beside his friend and hears his anger, fear, and regret.

The heroic pair can no longer act together against an outside enemy. Gilgamesh’s role changes from fellow warrior to powerless witness.

This helplessness prepares for the depth of his mourning after Enkidu dies.

10. Enkidu curses the hunter

Enkidu next curses the hunter who first discovered him at the watering place and reported his existence.

In Enkidu’s mind, the hunter began the sequence that removed him from the wilderness and led him toward Uruk, heroic adventure, divine punishment, and death.

He imagines that, without the hunter’s intervention, he might still be living safely among the animals.

11. Enkidu curses Shamhat

Enkidu then turns against Shamhat, the woman who guided him into human society.

He wishes hardship, rejection, and humiliation upon her. His curse reverses the gratitude and trust that previously existed between them.

Enkidu now interprets civilization entirely through the suffering it has brought him. The gains of friendship and knowledge temporarily disappear beneath his fear of death.

12. Why Enkidu blames Shamhat

Shamhat did not sentence Enkidu to death. However, she represents the irreversible transition that made his present life possible.

Without her, he would not have entered Uruk, met Gilgamesh, travelled to the Cedar Forest, or fought the Bull of Heaven.

His curse expresses the painful logic that every human gain also made his later losses possible.

13. Shamash speaks from heaven

Shamash hears Enkidu’s curse and responds. He challenges Enkidu’s claim that Shamhat only harmed him.

Shamash reminds him that Shamhat gave him bread, beer, clothing, dignity, and entry into human society.

Most importantly, she led him to Gilgamesh, who became his closest friend and who will mourn him deeply.

14. Shamash reframes Enkidu’s life

Shamash does not deny that Enkidu will die. Instead, he asks Enkidu to judge his life as a whole rather than only by its ending.

Enkidu’s human life contains suffering, but it also contains love, honour, achievement, community, and memory.

The question becomes whether mortality makes those experiences meaningless or gives them greater value.

15. Enkidu withdraws the curse

After hearing Shamash, Enkidu changes his mind. He stops cursing Shamhat and gives her a blessing instead.

The reversal shows that Enkidu can recognize the complexity of his transformation. Civilization did not simply destroy him, nor did it simply improve him.

It brought both loss and extraordinary human connection.

16. Enkidu dreams of the underworld

Enkidu has another dream in which a terrifying being seizes him and transforms him into a creature suited to the land of the dead.

He is taken toward the House of Dust, the Mesopotamian underworld. The dream gives him a vision of the destination awaiting all mortal people.

Unlike his earlier dream of the divine council, this dream does not merely announce judgment. It imagines the actual experience of entering death.

17. The terrifying bird-like figure

Enkidu describes a powerful being with features associated with a bird of prey. It attacks him, overcomes his strength, and carries him away.

The figure demonstrates that Enkidu’s heroic body is useless before death. He cannot wrestle free as he once wrestled Gilgamesh.

His transformation also symbolizes the loss of his living human identity.

18. Enkidu enters the House of Dust

The underworld is described as a dark house from which those who enter do not ordinarily return.

Its inhabitants live in darkness and consume dust or clay-like food. Their former worldly power has not protected them.

The imagery presents death as a reduction of human vitality, identity, pleasure, and social distinction.

19. Kings and powerful people appear among the dead

Enkidu sees rulers, priests, and other important figures in the underworld.

Their presence is significant because death does not spare those who possessed authority, wealth, or sacred status during life.

The vision anticipates Gilgamesh’s later realization that kingship and extraordinary strength cannot exempt him from mortality.

20. The underworld reverses earthly hierarchy

In life, Gilgamesh and Enkidu gained fame by distinguishing themselves from ordinary people.

In Enkidu’s dream, death gathers rulers and common people into the same dark condition.

The underworld therefore challenges the heroic belief that greatness can completely overcome human limitation.

21. Enkidu’s illness continues

Enkidu remains sick for a prolonged period. The poem counts the days as his suffering increases.

This slow decline is very different from the sudden deaths of enemies in battle.

The delay forces both Enkidu and Gilgamesh to experience the approach of death consciously.

22. Enkidu compares his death with dying in battle

Enkidu regrets that he will not die in heroic combat. He imagines that death in battle might have brought greater honour.

His illness seems passive and humiliating because it gives him no enemy to confront and no final display of strength.

The complaint reveals how heroic culture can value some forms of death more than others, even though all deaths remain final.

23. Gilgamesh remains with Enkidu

Gilgamesh does not abandon his friend. He listens to Enkidu’s dreams, anger, regrets, and fears.

Yet companionship cannot cure the illness or reverse the gods’ decree.

Friendship is emotionally powerful but not powerful enough to defeat mortality.

24. Enkidu dies

After days of illness, Enkidu dies. The companion created to restrain and transform Gilgamesh is removed from his life.

Enkidu’s death ends the heroic partnership that structured the first half of the epic.

It also forces Gilgamesh to confront a truth he previously treated mainly as an idea: if Enkidu can die, Gilgamesh must eventually die as well. Tablet VII concludes with Enkidu’s death. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

Difficult Ideas Explained

Why is Enkidu punished when Gilgamesh participated too?

The epic does not provide a fully satisfying human explanation. Both companions share responsibility, but Enkidu is selected by the divine council.

His death may preserve Gilgamesh so that Gilgamesh can complete the transformation and search that form the rest of the epic. Within the story, however, the decision can still appear unequal.

Did the gods previously approve the heroes’ actions?

Shamash certainly helped them against Humbaba, but the gods do not operate as one perfectly unified authority.

Assistance from one god does not guarantee approval from every god or protection from later consequences.

Why does Enkidu curse the cedar door?

The door is a physical product of the Cedar Forest expedition. It represents the victory that has now become connected with his punishment.

Enkidu cannot attack the gods or reverse fate, so he directs his anger toward a visible symbol of his actions.

Why does Enkidu curse Shamhat?

He imagines a chain of causes: Shamhat led him into civilization, civilization led him to Gilgamesh, and Gilgamesh led him into the actions for which he will die.

The curse comes from grief and fear rather than from a balanced judgment of everything Shamhat gave him.

Why does Shamash defend Shamhat?

Shamash reminds Enkidu that human life brought him more than death. It also brought friendship, honour, food, clothing, community, and a life that people will remember.

Why does Enkidu change the curse into a blessing?

Shamash helps him recognize that his life cannot be judged only by its final suffering.

Enkidu accepts that Shamhat gave him access to experiences he would never have known in the wilderness.

Is the underworld dream a literal description?

Within the epic, it presents a Mesopotamian image of the condition of the dead. It also functions psychologically by giving form to Enkidu’s fear of losing life, identity, status, and bodily power.

Why are kings also found in the underworld?

Their presence emphasizes that political or heroic greatness does not remove mortality.

This lesson is especially important for Gilgamesh, whose royal and partly divine identity has made him appear almost beyond ordinary limits.

Why does Enkidu prefer death in battle?

Heroic culture associates battle death with courage and public honour. Illness appears passive because Enkidu cannot resist it or transform it into a visible achievement.

Why does the illness last for several days?

The slow decline prevents Enkidu’s death from becoming another brief action scene.

It gives him time to fear, regret, reinterpret his life, and say farewell while forcing Gilgamesh to witness his helplessness.

Major Themes in Tablet VII

Divine judgment and human fairness

The gods possess the authority to determine Enkidu’s fate, but their decision does not necessarily appear fair from a human perspective.

The consequences of heroic violence

The killings of Humbaba and the Bull of Heaven return as causes of punishment. Victory does not erase responsibility.

Anger against fate

Enkidu cannot fight death directly, so he attacks the door, hunter, and Shamhat as symbols of the path that led him there.

Civilization as gain and loss

Enkidu’s curse emphasizes what civilization cost him, while Shamash’s response emphasizes what it gave him.

The epic refuses to reduce his transformation to either pure progress or pure corruption.

The value of a mortal life

Shamash’s argument suggests that a life can remain meaningful even when it ends in death.

Friendship, memory, honour, and human experience may matter precisely because they are temporary.

The equality created by death

Enkidu’s vision of the underworld places kings and ordinary people under the same mortal condition.

The limits of physical power

Enkidu’s strength defeated enemies but cannot defeat disease, divine judgment, or death.

Friendship and helplessness

Gilgamesh can remain beside Enkidu, but he cannot save him. Friendship provides love and witness rather than control over fate.

The transition from adventure to grief

Enkidu’s death ends the heroic partnership and begins Gilgamesh’s emotional and philosophical crisis.

Why Tablet VII Matters

Tablet VII is the structural and emotional turning point of The Epic of Gilgamesh. The earlier tablets followed the creation of friendship and the heroes’ growing power. This tablet destroys that partnership.

Enkidu’s illness also changes the epic’s treatment of death. Earlier, Gilgamesh accepted death as a reason to seek fame. Enkidu’s decline reveals death as physical suffering, separation, loss of identity, and the end of companionship.

The tablet also completes Enkidu’s personal journey. He first rejects his human life in anger, then recognizes that Shamhat and civilization gave him experiences worth having despite their consequences.

Enkidu’s death now leaves Gilgamesh with a problem no heroic battle can solve. The remainder of the epic will follow his attempt to understand or escape the mortality he sees reflected in his friend.

Questions to Consider

  • Why do the gods choose Enkidu rather than Gilgamesh?
  • Can the gods fairly punish actions that Shamash helped the heroes perform?
  • Why does Enkidu direct his anger toward objects and people rather than toward Gilgamesh?
  • Is Enkidu right to view Shamhat as the cause of his death?
  • Does Shamash successfully prove that Enkidu’s human life was worth its suffering?
  • What does Enkidu gain by changing his curse into a blessing?
  • Why does the underworld contain rulers and powerful religious figures?
  • What is more frightening to Enkidu: physical pain, loss of status, separation from Gilgamesh, or nonexistence?
  • Why does Enkidu consider death from illness less honourable than death in battle?
  • How will Enkidu’s death change Gilgamesh’s understanding of his own life?
