Tablet IX: The Wanderings of Gilgamesh

Gilgamesh leaves Uruk in terror of death, crosses the wilderness, reaches Mount Mashu, and passes through the path of the sun in search of Uta-napishti and the secret of immortality.

Tablet IX at a Glance

Tablet IX begins after Enkidu’s funeral. Gilgamesh is no longer only mourning his friend. Enkidu’s death has made him recognize that the same fate awaits him, and this realization fills him with terror.

He leaves Uruk and wanders through the wilderness wearing animal skins. The civilized king who once stood proudly within his city now becomes physically and psychologically disordered.

Gilgamesh decides to seek Uta-napishti, the survivor of the great flood who was granted immortality. He hopes to learn how a human being might escape death.

His journey takes him to Mount Mashu, the twin mountain associated with the sun’s rising and setting. Its entrance is guarded by terrifying beings who are part human and part scorpion.

After persuading the guardians to let him pass, Gilgamesh enters the path travelled by the sun. He moves through complete darkness for many hours before finally emerging into light and reaching a miraculous garden where precious stones grow like fruit.

Tablet IX transforms Gilgamesh’s journey from heroic conquest into desperate spiritual and existential searching. He is no longer seeking fame. He is seeking literal freedom from mortality.

Detailed Sequence of Events

1. Gilgamesh wanders after Enkidu’s death

After completing Enkidu’s funeral rites, Gilgamesh leaves the ordered life of Uruk and wanders through open country.

His movement has no heroic destination at first. It expresses grief, confusion, and the inability to return to his previous life.

The king who once possessed a city, subjects, monuments, and royal authority now appears isolated and directionless.

2. Grief becomes fear of personal death

Gilgamesh repeatedly remembers Enkidu and recognizes that his own body is subject to the same fate.

Earlier in the epic, Gilgamesh accepted that humans die and tried to answer mortality by winning fame. Enkidu’s death destroys that confidence.

Death is no longer an abstract fact about humanity. It has taken the person closest to him and revealed what will eventually happen to Gilgamesh himself.

3. Gilgamesh asks whether he too must die

Gilgamesh’s thoughts repeatedly return to one question: will he become like Enkidu?

The answer is clearly yes, but emotionally he cannot accept it. His search begins as an attempt to find an exception to the normal human condition.

This is different from courageous acceptance. Gilgamesh is driven by panic and refusal.

4. Gilgamesh adopts a wild appearance

Gilgamesh wears animal skins and allows his body and clothing to become rough and neglected.

This reverses his earlier royal presentation. In Tablet VI he washed, arranged his hair, and dressed magnificently. After Enkidu’s death, he abandons the outward signs of civilization and kingship.

His appearance also connects him with Enkidu’s original wilderness identity. Gilgamesh seems to carry part of his dead friend’s former life into his own wandering.

5. Gilgamesh leaves the security of Uruk

Uruk represents walls, community, political responsibility, and lasting human achievement. Gilgamesh turns away from all of these.

He is not satisfied with the kind of permanence the city can offer. He wants his own individual body to continue living.

His departure therefore shows that grief has temporarily made him reject the human answer to mortality that the epic will later restore.

6. Gilgamesh seeks Uta-napishti

Gilgamesh has heard of Uta-napishti, a man who survived the great flood and was granted everlasting life by the gods.

Uta-napishti appears to prove that death is not completely unavoidable. If one human became immortal, Gilgamesh hopes that the same possibility might exist for him.

However, Gilgamesh does not yet understand that Uta-napishti’s fate was an exceptional divine decision rather than a method that can easily be repeated.

7. He travels alone through dangerous country

Gilgamesh crosses remote regions without Enkidu beside him.

Earlier journeys depended on their partnership. They encouraged one another, interpreted dreams, shared physical danger, and fought as a pair.

The loneliness of Tablet IX emphasizes that Enkidu cannot be replaced. Gilgamesh must now face fear without his former equal and protector.

8. Gilgamesh encounters lions at night

While travelling, Gilgamesh sees lions in the wilderness and becomes afraid.

He prays for divine assistance and then fights the animals. The precise sequence is partly difficult because the surviving text is damaged, but Gilgamesh survives the encounter.

The scene shows that his physical courage remains, even though his deeper fear of death has become overwhelming.

9. The lions reflect Gilgamesh’s unstable condition

Lions are wild, dangerous, and powerful. Gilgamesh both fears them and wears their skins.

The encounter mirrors his own transformation into a figure moving between human order and animal wilderness.

Killing lions proves that he can still defeat immediate physical threats, but it does nothing to solve the mortality that truly terrifies him.

10. Gilgamesh reaches Mount Mashu

After prolonged wandering, Gilgamesh reaches the twin mountain called Mashu.

The mountain is associated with the route of the sun. It guards the place where the sun enters and leaves the world during its daily movement.

Mashu therefore stands at the boundary between the ordinary human world and a cosmic region beyond normal experience.

11. Mount Mashu connects heaven and the underworld

The mountain is described as extending upward toward the heavens and downward toward the world below.

Its enormous scale makes it more than a geographical feature. It functions as a cosmic gateway connecting different levels of existence.

To continue, Gilgamesh must cross a boundary normally associated with divine or supernatural movement.

12. The entrance is guarded by Scorpion Beings

Gilgamesh encounters a male and female pair of terrifying guardians whose bodies combine human and scorpion features.

Their appearance is so overwhelming that merely looking at them is described as dangerous.

They guard the route used by the sun and prevent ordinary travellers from entering.

13. Gilgamesh is afraid but does not flee

The Scorpion Beings terrify Gilgamesh, but he gathers enough courage to approach them.

This scene again distinguishes courage from fearlessness. Gilgamesh continues even though he understands that the guardians possess power far beyond his own.

Unlike his approach to Humbaba, however, he does not immediately attempt violence. He speaks and explains his purpose.

14. The Scorpion Man recognizes Gilgamesh’s divine nature

The guardian observes that Gilgamesh is not entirely ordinary. His body and ancestry contain a divine element.

Yet Gilgamesh’s partly divine nature has not protected him from grief or made him immortal.

The recognition emphasizes the central contradiction of his identity: he is greater than ordinary humans but still subject to human death.

15. The guardians notice Gilgamesh’s damaged appearance

The Scorpion Beings question why someone of such extraordinary status looks exhausted, thin, and dressed in animal skins.

Gilgamesh’s outward appearance displays his internal suffering. Grief has physically transformed the once magnificent king.

His body has become evidence of the cost of mourning and wandering.

16. Gilgamesh explains Enkidu’s death

Gilgamesh tells the guardians about Enkidu, their friendship, and the death that caused his journey.

He repeatedly introduces himself through his loss. Enkidu remains central to his identity even after death.

The explanation also makes clear that Gilgamesh is not travelling from curiosity. He is fleeing the fate he saw in his friend.

17. Gilgamesh asks for passage

Gilgamesh asks to enter the mountain and follow the road toward Uta-napishti.

The request is extraordinary because no ordinary human is expected to travel through the sun’s path.

His willingness to attempt it shows the intensity of his desperation.

18. The guardian warns that the route is impossible

The Scorpion Man explains that the passage is extremely long and filled with complete darkness.

No light reaches the path while the traveller is inside. The route removes ordinary sight, direction, and security.

The warning makes the journey sound less like normal travel and more like a symbolic passage through death itself.

19. Gilgamesh insists on continuing

Despite the warning, Gilgamesh refuses to turn back.

His persistence can be understood as courage, but also as obsession. He is willing to cross a route associated with cosmic danger because he cannot accept mortality.

Grief gives him extraordinary determination while also narrowing his judgment.

20. The Scorpion Beings open the gate

The guardians eventually allow Gilgamesh to enter the mountain.

Their permission distinguishes this episode from the Cedar Forest. Gilgamesh does not invade by force or kill the guardians.

He gains passage through explanation, recognition, and consent.

21. Gilgamesh enters the path of the sun

Gilgamesh begins travelling through the tunnel or passage used by the sun.

Once inside, he is surrounded by complete darkness. He cannot see what lies ahead or behind him.

The journey tests endurance rather than combat. There is no enemy to strike and no visible destination to guide him.

22. The poem measures the journey in double-hours

The text repeatedly marks the passage of time as Gilgamesh moves through successive stages.

Each stage seems nearly identical because the darkness removes ordinary landmarks.

The repetition creates pressure and claustrophobia. Progress exists, but Gilgamesh cannot visually confirm it.

23. Darkness surrounds him completely

The poem repeatedly emphasizes that Gilgamesh can see nothing in front of or behind himself.

This darkness can be read literally as the sunless interior of the mountain and symbolically as Gilgamesh’s grief and uncertainty.

He is moving without knowledge, guided only by determination.

24. Gilgamesh continues without Enkidu

The tunnel intensifies Gilgamesh’s solitude. No companion interprets signs, guards him, or reassures him.

In the Cedar Forest journey, fear was managed through conversation. Here Gilgamesh must endure silence and darkness alone.

The journey therefore dramatizes the isolation produced by death: the living person must continue where the dead companion cannot.

25. The darkness begins to change

After many stages, Gilgamesh senses that the end of the passage is approaching.

The first signs of light represent relief after the oppressive darkness.

However, reaching light does not mean that he has found immortality. It only means that he has crossed one barrier.

26. Gilgamesh races against the sun

The timing of the passage is connected with the sun’s daily movement. Gilgamesh must escape before the sun enters the route.

This creates urgency. He is not merely travelling through darkness; he is moving through a cosmic path that does not belong to humans.

His successful crossing again marks him as extraordinary without making him immortal.

27. Gilgamesh emerges into light

Gilgamesh finally exits the tunnel and enters a brilliantly lit region beyond Mount Mashu.

The transition from absolute darkness to radiance resembles a form of rebirth.

Yet Gilgamesh’s central fear remains unresolved. He has survived a death-like journey, but he is still mortal.

28. Gilgamesh sees the garden of precious stones

Beyond the mountain, Gilgamesh discovers a supernatural garden in which trees bear jewels and precious stones instead of ordinary fruit.

The garden is beautiful, abundant, and unlike the human world. It signals that Gilgamesh has reached the distant edge of ordinary geography.

The scene offers wonder after darkness, but its riches do not answer his search for life.

29. The garden contrasts natural life with material permanence

Ordinary fruit grows, ripens, and decays. The garden’s stone fruit appears permanent and incorruptible.

This makes it appropriate to Gilgamesh’s search for something that does not die.

However, precious stones possess durability without life. Their permanence is different from the living immortality Gilgamesh wants.

30. Gilgamesh has reached the edge of the human world

By the end of Tablet IX, Gilgamesh has crossed wilderness, monsters, mountains, darkness, and the path of the sun.

His journey proves extraordinary endurance, but it has not yet given him the secret of immortality.

Tablet X will continue his journey through the strange region at the edge of the world and bring him closer to Uta-napishti.

Difficult Ideas Explained

Why does Gilgamesh leave Uruk?

Uruk represents ordinary human permanence through buildings, community, kingship, and memory. Gilgamesh is no longer satisfied with that answer.

He wants to prevent his individual death, so he abandons the city and searches for a literal form of immortality.

Why does Gilgamesh wear animal skins?

The clothing reflects his separation from civilized royal life and his movement into the wilderness.

It may also connect him symbolically with Enkidu, who originally lived among animals.

Why is Gilgamesh suddenly afraid of death?

He always knew that human beings die, but Enkidu’s death makes that knowledge emotionally real.

Gilgamesh now sees his own future in the decay of his friend’s body.

Why does he believe Uta-napishti can help?

Uta-napishti was once human and received immortality after surviving the flood.

Gilgamesh assumes that Uta-napishti may know a path that another human can follow, though he does not yet understand how exceptional Uta-napishti’s case was.

What is Mount Mashu?

Mount Mashu is a cosmic mountain associated with the sun’s rising and setting.

It functions as a gateway between the ordinary human world and the distant supernatural region Gilgamesh must cross.

Who are the Scorpion Beings?

They are supernatural guardians with both human and scorpion characteristics.

Their role is to protect the sun’s route and prevent unauthorized passage into a region beyond normal human access.

Why do they allow Gilgamesh through?

They recognize his extraordinary partly divine identity and hear the reason for his journey.

Gilgamesh also approaches them through speech rather than attack.

Is the tunnel a journey through the underworld?

It is primarily the dark route travelled by the sun through Mount Mashu.

However, its total darkness, isolation, cosmic location, and emergence into light give it strong symbolic similarities to death and rebirth.

Why is the journey measured repeatedly?

The repeated time markers show the great length of the passage and create suspense.

Because Gilgamesh cannot see landmarks, time becomes the only measure of progress.

What does the darkness symbolize?

It may represent grief, fear, lack of knowledge, and the condition of moving forward without certainty.

Gilgamesh knows what he is escaping but does not yet understand what he will find.

Why does the garden grow jewels?

The garden marks a supernatural region beyond ordinary agriculture and human life.

Its permanent stone fruit contrasts with mortal living bodies, which grow and decay.

Does reaching the garden mean Gilgamesh has succeeded?

No. He has crossed an extraordinary barrier, but he has not escaped mortality or even reached Uta-napishti yet.

The beautiful garden is another stage of the search, not its answer.

Major Themes in Tablet IX

Grief transformed into fear

Gilgamesh’s sorrow for Enkidu becomes terror that the same death will happen to him.

The rejection of ordinary human life

Gilgamesh abandons Uruk because he temporarily rejects community, kingship, memory, and achievement as adequate responses to death.

Civilization reversed

Enkidu moved from wilderness into civilization. After Enkidu’s death, Gilgamesh moves from civilization into wilderness.

Physical strength versus existential fear

Gilgamesh can kill lions and cross dangerous landscapes, but these feats cannot defeat the idea of death.

The loneliness of survival

Gilgamesh must continue without Enkidu, making the journey an experience of emotional isolation.

Crossing forbidden boundaries

Gilgamesh attempts to enter spaces associated with gods, cosmic movement, and forces beyond ordinary humanity.

Darkness and uncertainty

The sun’s passage represents progress without sight, knowledge, or reassurance.

Endurance without understanding

Gilgamesh survives because he continues moving, but persistence does not yet produce wisdom.

Permanence without life

The garden’s precious stones appear incorruptible, but they are not alive. The scene distinguishes durability from true immortality.

The hero as seeker rather than conqueror

Gilgamesh does not overcome Mashu through violence. He persuades its guardians and endures its path, marking a new kind of heroic journey.

Why Tablet IX Matters

Tablet IX begins the second major movement of The Epic of Gilgamesh. The first half followed the creation of friendship and the pursuit of heroic fame. The second follows Gilgamesh’s attempt to escape death itself.

The tablet also shows how completely Enkidu’s death has transformed Gilgamesh. The king of Uruk becomes a solitary wanderer dressed in animal skins, moving through landscapes beyond human civilization.

Gilgamesh’s journey through Mount Mashu is important because it resembles a passage through death. He enters complete darkness, loses ordinary orientation, endures isolation, and emerges again into light.

Yet this symbolic rebirth does not make him immortal. It only carries him farther from the human world and closer to the truth he still resists: extraordinary effort cannot automatically overturn the limits placed on human life.

Questions to Consider

  • Why is Gilgamesh unable to return to normal life after Enkidu’s funeral?
  • What does his animal-skin clothing reveal about his psychological condition?
  • Why is fame no longer enough for Gilgamesh?
  • Is his search for Uta-napishti courageous, irrational, or both?
  • Why does the poem make Gilgamesh travel without a companion?
  • What is the difference between defeating lions and defeating death?
  • Why does Gilgamesh speak to the Scorpion Beings rather than fight them?
  • What might the path of complete darkness symbolize?
  • Does emerging into light represent genuine transformation or only survival?
  • Why does a garden of permanent stones appear during a search for eternal life?
