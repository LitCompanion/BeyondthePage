Characters and Relationships

A guide to the major figures in The Epic of Gilgamesh, their development, actions, and relationships throughout the story.

← Back to The Epic of Gilgamesh

Main Characters

Gilgamesh

Gilgamesh is the king of Uruk and the central figure of the epic. He begins as an extraordinarily powerful but oppressive ruler whose behavior causes suffering among his people.

His relationship with Enkidu changes him. Through their friendship, Gilgamesh experiences loyalty, ambition, violence, grief, and fear. After Enkidu dies, he becomes terrified of his own mortality and begins searching for a way to escape death.

By the end of the epic, Gilgamesh learns that immortality is not available to ordinary humans. His return to Uruk suggests a greater acceptance of human limits and of the lasting value of human achievements.

Enkidu

Enkidu is created as someone strong enough to confront Gilgamesh. He begins in the wilderness, living among animals and outside human society.

After meeting Shamhat, he enters civilization and learns human customs, food, clothing, language, and social behavior. He loses his former connection with the animals but gains friendship, knowledge, and a place in human society.

Enkidu first meets Gilgamesh as a rival, but their confrontation develops into a close friendship. Together they kill Humbaba and the Bull of Heaven. Enkidu’s death becomes the major turning point of the epic and begins Gilgamesh’s search for immortality.

Shamhat

Shamhat is the woman sent to meet Enkidu in the wilderness. She becomes the person who introduces him to human intimacy, language, clothing, food, and society.

Her role is important because Enkidu’s transformation is both a gain and a loss. He becomes fully human and socially connected, but he can no longer return to his previous life among the animals.

Ninsun

Ninsun is Gilgamesh’s mother and one of his most important advisers. She interprets his dreams and recognizes that Enkidu will become his companion.

Before the journey to the Cedar Forest, she prays for the safety of Gilgamesh and Enkidu. She also accepts Enkidu into her family, strengthening the bond between the two heroes.

Humbaba

Humbaba is the guardian of the Cedar Forest. Gilgamesh and Enkidu travel there in search of fame and kill him despite his pleas for mercy.

Although he appears as a terrifying enemy, his death raises an important question: are Gilgamesh and Enkidu acting heroically, or are they violating a sacred boundary for personal glory?

Utnapishtim

Utnapishtim is the survivor of the great flood and one of the only humans granted immortality. Gilgamesh seeks him out because he hopes to discover how he can also escape death.

Utnapishtim explains that his immortality was an exceptional gift, not a reward available to everyone. His story forces Gilgamesh to confront the difference between extraordinary divine exceptions and ordinary human limits.

Siduri

Siduri is a tavern keeper whom Gilgamesh meets during his search for Utnapishtim. She initially fears him because grief and exhaustion have transformed his appearance.

She questions his pursuit of immortality and directs him toward Urshanabi, who can help him continue his journey.

Urshanabi

Urshanabi is the boatman who helps Gilgamesh cross the dangerous waters leading to Utnapishtim.

He later returns with Gilgamesh to Uruk and is present when Gilgamesh draws attention to the city and its walls at the end of the epic.

Key Relationships

Gilgamesh and Enkidu

Their relationship begins in conflict. Enkidu is created partly to challenge Gilgamesh, and the two first meet as physical rivals. Their struggle quickly becomes mutual respect and then deep friendship.

Their bond gives Gilgamesh companionship and redirects his energy toward heroic adventures. Enkidu’s death then destroys Gilgamesh’s sense of security and forces him to recognize that he too will die.

Enkidu and Shamhat

Shamhat guides Enkidu from the wilderness into civilization. Their relationship marks the beginning of his transformation from an animal-like outsider into a social and self-aware human being.

Gilgamesh and Ninsun

Ninsun acts as Gilgamesh’s mother, interpreter, and adviser. She understands the significance of his dreams and provides a calmer, wiser perspective than Gilgamesh usually possesses.

Gilgamesh and Humbaba

Gilgamesh treats Humbaba as an obstacle to fame, but Humbaba is also the appointed guardian of a sacred place. Their conflict therefore complicates the idea of heroism.

Gilgamesh and Utnapishtim

Gilgamesh approaches Utnapishtim hoping to learn the secret of immortality. Instead, Utnapishtim teaches him that his own case was unique and cannot provide a normal path beyond death.
