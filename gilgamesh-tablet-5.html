Tablet V: The Combat with Humbaba

Gilgamesh and Enkidu enter the Forest of Cedar, confront its guardian, receive divine assistance, and make a decision whose consequences will follow them for the rest of the epic.

Tablet V at a Glance

Tablet V brings Gilgamesh and Enkidu into the Forest of Cedar after the long preparation and journey of the previous tablets. The heroes admire the forest’s height, beauty, fragrance, and abundance before confronting Humbaba, its divinely appointed guardian.

When Humbaba appears, his terrifying presence shakes the confidence of the heroes. Gilgamesh and Enkidu must encourage one another to continue. During the battle, Shamash sends powerful winds that trap Humbaba and prevent him from escaping.

Once defeated, Humbaba begs Gilgamesh to spare him and offers to become his servant. Gilgamesh briefly hesitates, but Enkidu insists that Humbaba must be killed before the gods can intervene. The heroes kill him, cut down the cedars, and begin transforming the sacred forest into material for human use.

The victory brings the fame Gilgamesh desired, but the tablet also makes the achievement morally uncertain. Humbaba is dangerous, yet he is also the guardian of a sacred place. The heroes’ success may therefore also be an act of destruction and violation.

Detailed Sequence of Events

1. The heroes enter the Forest of Cedar

Gilgamesh and Enkidu finally reach the destination that has occupied them for several tablets. They stand at the edge of the forest and observe the road, the cedar trees, and the path used by Humbaba.

The forest is not introduced merely as a battlefield. It is described as an impressive and living environment filled with towering trees, rich scents, wildlife, and sounds.

This beauty complicates the expedition. The heroes have described Humbaba’s territory mainly as a place of danger, but the tablet reveals that it is also ordered, abundant, and sacred.

2. The forest appears both beautiful and threatening

The heroes admire the height of the cedars and the paths through the forest. The natural world seems grander than ordinary human spaces and exists beyond the direct control of Uruk.

At the same time, the forest’s size and density create uncertainty. The heroes have entered a place governed by forces they do not fully understand.

The landscape therefore produces both wonder and fear. Its beauty does not make it safe, and its danger does not make it worthless.

3. The heroes cut a cedar and attract Humbaba’s attention

Gilgamesh and Enkidu begin cutting cedar trees. This action announces their presence and directly challenges Humbaba’s authority as guardian of the forest.

The cutting is not a neutral act. The forest has been placed under divine protection, so damaging it turns the heroes from visitors into intruders.

Their desire for timber, fame, and heroic achievement therefore brings them into conflict with a sacred boundary.

4. Humbaba confronts Gilgamesh and Enkidu

Humbaba appears and challenges the heroes. His presence is described through overwhelming sound, force, and terrifying supernatural qualities.

He is not merely a large warrior. He possesses a power connected to the forest and to the divine authority that appointed him.

The warnings given by Enkidu and the elders are now confirmed. Gilgamesh is facing an opponent whose power exceeds ordinary human experience.

5. Humbaba attacks the heroes psychologically

Humbaba does not rely only on physical strength. He insults, intimidates, and attempts to divide Gilgamesh and Enkidu.

He questions Enkidu’s loyalty and reminds him of his former life in the wilderness. Because Enkidu once knew the forest and feared its guardian, Humbaba tries to use that knowledge against him.

The confrontation therefore becomes psychological as well as physical. Humbaba attempts to weaken the friendship that gives the heroes courage.

6. Gilgamesh becomes afraid

When Humbaba’s full power becomes apparent, Gilgamesh’s confidence weakens. The hero who initiated the expedition now confronts the reality behind the warnings he previously dismissed.

His fear does not contradict his heroism. As in Tablet IV, courage depends on continuing despite terror rather than never experiencing it.

The scene also shows that ambition formed at a distance is different from action taken in the immediate presence of danger.

7. Enkidu encourages Gilgamesh

Enkidu reminds Gilgamesh of their journey, their promises, and their shared strength. He urges him not to retreat now that they have reached their goal.

Earlier, Enkidu was the one who feared Humbaba and warned against the expedition. Now he becomes the more aggressive and determined member of the pair.

Their roles continue to shift. Each man supports the other when fear threatens to overcome him.

8. Gilgamesh calls upon Shamash

Gilgamesh appeals to Shamash, the god whose protection Ninsun sought before the expedition.

The prayer acknowledges that the heroes cannot defeat Humbaba through physical strength alone.

The moment connects Tablet V with the preparations in Tablet III: Ninsun’s ritual request for divine assistance now becomes decisive.

9. Shamash sends the winds

Shamash sends a series of powerful winds against Humbaba. They strike him from different directions and prevent him from moving forward, retreating, or escaping.

The winds change the balance of the battle. Humbaba’s defeat is not achieved by Gilgamesh and Enkidu alone but through direct divine intervention.

This complicates the meaning of their victory. They are heroic fighters, but their success depends on assistance beyond human power.

10. Humbaba loses his ability to resist

Trapped by the winds, Humbaba can no longer use his strength or flee deeper into the forest.

The terrifying guardian becomes vulnerable. The battle shifts from combat between active opponents to a decision about what should be done with a defeated enemy.

At this point, the central question is no longer whether Gilgamesh can defeat Humbaba, but whether he should kill him.

11. Humbaba begs Gilgamesh for mercy

Humbaba praises Gilgamesh, appeals to his royal identity, and offers to serve him. He promises access to the forest and its cedars if his life is spared.

His speech transforms him from a distant monster into a speaking, reasoning figure who fears death.

The plea is significant because the epic does not allow the killing to appear completely uncomplicated. Humbaba is dangerous, but he is also defeated, helpless, and capable of asking for mercy.

12. Gilgamesh begins to hesitate

Gilgamesh appears willing to consider Humbaba’s offer. Mercy could bring practical advantages, including access to cedar timber and the service of a powerful guardian.

His hesitation may also show that the defeated Humbaba no longer seems identical to the terrifying monster imagined before the battle.

Gilgamesh momentarily moves from heroic aggression toward judgment, but he does not make the decision alone.

13. Enkidu argues that Humbaba must die

Enkidu strongly opposes mercy. He warns that Humbaba may recover his strength or that the gods may intervene if the heroes delay.

Enkidu’s position is practical and fearful: leaving such a powerful enemy alive could place them in danger.

However, his insistence also makes him responsible for pushing Gilgamesh toward an irreversible act.

14. Humbaba curses Enkidu

Realizing that Enkidu is preventing Gilgamesh from sparing him, Humbaba turns against him and pronounces a curse.

The curse anticipates Enkidu’s later death and connects his fate to the killing in the forest.

This does not necessarily mean Humbaba alone causes Enkidu’s death, but it gives the episode a strong sense of approaching consequence.

15. Gilgamesh and Enkidu kill Humbaba

The heroes strike and kill the guardian. Their shared action completes the goal that motivated the expedition.

From one perspective, the killing is a heroic victory over a terrifying supernatural opponent.

From another perspective, it is the destruction of a divinely appointed guardian who had already been defeated and asked for mercy.

16. The forest reacts to Humbaba’s death

Humbaba’s death affects more than the two heroes. The forest and its creatures are disturbed by the loss of their guardian.

This reaction suggests that Humbaba was part of an ecological and sacred order, not simply an isolated monster.

Removing him leaves the forest exposed to human destruction.

17. The heroes cut down the cedars

After killing Humbaba, Gilgamesh and Enkidu continue cutting the trees. The forest is transformed from a sacred protected space into a source of material.

Cedar timber was valuable because of its strength, size, fragrance, and relative scarcity in southern Mesopotamia.

The cutting therefore represents human expansion into a distant natural resource, but the poem also allows the reader to recognize the violence of that transformation.

18. They construct a great door or gate

Enkidu selects an especially large cedar and prepares it as a door intended for a major temple or sacred building.

The action turns the conquered forest into a monument of human religious and political culture.

It also creates an irony: the heroes violate a sacred forest and then use its timber for another sacred structure.

19. The heroes prepare to return

With Humbaba dead and the cedars cut, Gilgamesh and Enkidu prepare to leave the forest and transport the timber toward Uruk.

They have achieved the fame-producing deed Gilgamesh desired.

However, the poem has already planted signs that the victory will have consequences: Humbaba’s curse, the disturbance of the forest, and the heroes’ violation of divine authority.

Difficult Ideas Explained

Is Humbaba an evil monster?

Humbaba is frightening and dangerous, but he is also the appointed guardian of the forest. His task is to prevent unauthorized people from entering and cutting the cedars.

The epic therefore presents him as both an opponent and a defender of a protected place.

Why does Shamash help the heroes kill a divinely appointed guardian?

Mesopotamian gods do not always act according to one united plan. Shamash supports Gilgamesh even though Humbaba’s authority is also connected to the divine world.

The episode illustrates disagreement and competing loyalties among the gods rather than a single perfectly consistent moral system.

What are Humbaba’s supernatural powers?

Humbaba is associated with terrifying radiance, sound, force, and protective powers sometimes described as auras or garments.

Because the surviving text is damaged and reconstructed from several sources, the exact operation of these powers is not always completely clear.

Why does Humbaba offer to serve Gilgamesh?

He attempts to save his life by appealing to Gilgamesh’s pride and royal ambition. A powerful guardian and access to cedar timber would both be valuable to a king.

Why does Enkidu insist on killing Humbaba?

Enkidu fears that Humbaba could recover, escape, or receive help if they hesitate. He also knows the guardian’s power more directly than Gilgamesh does.

Yet his advice prevents Gilgamesh from considering mercy and makes Enkidu partly responsible for the consequences.

Why is Humbaba’s curse important?

The curse links the killing to Enkidu’s later fate. It creates the expectation that heroic violence will not end when the battle ends.

Are Gilgamesh and Enkidu heroes or destroyers?

The tablet supports both interpretations. They display courage, loyalty, endurance, and extraordinary strength.

At the same time, they invade a sacred space, kill its defeated guardian, and begin cutting down the forest he protected.

Why is the description of the forest important?

The detailed natural description prevents the forest from appearing as an empty setting. It is a complex living environment whose destruction has meaning beyond the heroes’ personal adventure.

Major Themes in Tablet V

Heroism and moral uncertainty

The heroes accomplish a dangerous deed, but the poem does not make the morality of their victory completely simple.

Nature and human expansion

The protected forest becomes a resource to be conquered, cut, and transported for human construction.

Mercy and irreversible choice

Once Humbaba is trapped, the heroes face a choice between mercy and execution. Killing him cannot later be undone.

Friendship and dangerous influence

Gilgamesh and Enkidu give one another courage, but their friendship can also intensify reckless decisions. Enkidu’s support helps Gilgamesh act, even when restraint might be wiser.

Divine assistance and human responsibility

Shamash makes victory possible, but Gilgamesh and Enkidu still choose what to do with the defeated Humbaba.

Fame and consequence

The heroes gain the fame they sought, but their achievement also produces curses, divine anger, and future loss.

Sacred boundaries

The forest is not ordinary territory. Entering, damaging, and exploiting it becomes a challenge to an order established beyond human authority.

Why Tablet V Matters

Tablet V completes the first major expedition of the epic. Gilgamesh proves that he can perform the extraordinary deed he imagined, and Enkidu proves himself an essential guide, fighter, and companion.

At the same time, the tablet changes the meaning of heroic success. Humbaba’s death is not presented only as a glorious victory. His plea for mercy, his divine appointment, and the destruction of the forest introduce doubt into the achievement.

The tablet also prepares for the tragedy that follows. Enkidu’s insistence on killing Humbaba, Humbaba’s curse, and the violation of the sacred forest contribute to the growing conflict between the heroes and the divine order.

Questions to Consider

  • Does the beauty of the Cedar Forest change how we judge the expedition?
  • Is Humbaba primarily a monster, a victim, a guardian, or some combination of these?
  • Why does Gilgamesh hesitate when Humbaba asks for mercy?
  • Is Enkidu’s advice practical, cruel, fearful, or all three?
  • How much credit can the heroes claim when Shamash makes their victory possible?
  • Does divine assistance mean that the killing is morally approved?
  • What is lost when the forest is turned into timber?
  • Does friendship make Gilgamesh and Enkidu wiser, or does it sometimes make them more reckless?
  • At what moment does the expedition change from heroic achievement into possible violation?
