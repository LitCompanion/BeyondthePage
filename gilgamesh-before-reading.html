Why Context Matters

The Epic of Gilgamesh comes from a world very different from our own. Understanding its historical setting, religious beliefs, and literary development can make the characters and events much easier to follow.

Historical Background

Ancient Mesopotamia

The Epic of Gilgamesh comes from ancient Mesopotamia, a region located largely in present-day Iraq around the Tigris and Euphrates rivers. Mesopotamia was not a single nation with one government or one culture. Over thousands of years, it contained different peoples, languages, kingdoms, and independent city-states. Cities such as Uruk were political, economic, and religious centers surrounded by agricultural land. Their survival depended heavily on organized farming, irrigation, trade, labor, and systems of authority. 1

Uruk, the city ruled by Gilgamesh in the epic, became one of the earliest major urban centers in the ancient world. By around 3200 BCE, it contained monumental mud-brick buildings, religious institutions, workshops, and complex systems for organizing people and resources. The city was closely associated with the goddess Inanna, later known in Akkadian as Ishtar. Its temples and walls represented not only physical structures but also the collective power, identity, and achievements of its inhabitants. 1

This background is important because Uruk is more than the setting of the story. At the beginning of the epic, Gilgamesh possesses extraordinary strength and authority, but he uses his power in ways that cause suffering among his people. The citizens appeal to the gods, who create Enkidu as a possible equal and rival. The story therefore begins with a political question: possessing power does not automatically make someone a just or responsible king.

The contrast between Uruk and the wilderness is also central to the epic. Gilgamesh belongs to the city, while Enkidu initially lives among animals, outside agriculture, clothing, settled society, and human institutions. When Enkidu enters civilization, he gains food, clothing, friendship, knowledge, and participation in human society. At the same time, he loses his former connection with the animals and can no longer return completely to his earlier life. Civilization is therefore presented as both an achievement and a loss.

Mesopotamian religion also shaped how the original audience would have understood the story. The gods were connected with cities, natural forces, political authority, fertility, war, wisdom, and human destiny. They were immensely powerful, but they did not always agree with one another or act according to a single moral plan. Divine decisions could protect humans, punish them, or place limits on what they were permitted to achieve. This helps explain why the gods intervene so frequently in Gilgamesh’s journey.

The epic itself was not written by one author at one moment. Earlier independent Sumerian stories about Gilgamesh were later translated, combined, and reshaped into longer Akkadian versions. The text continued to change across centuries and survives on clay tablets and fragments from different periods and locations. Because many tablets are damaged or incomplete, modern translations may contain missing lines, brackets, or slightly different reconstructions of the same episode. 2

Keep in mind while reading

  • Does Gilgamesh become a better king as the story progresses?
  • What does Enkidu gain and lose when he enters human society?
  • Why are the walls and city of Uruk emphasized?
  • Are the gods presented as fair, unpredictable, or both?
  • How does the epic balance human achievement against human mortality?

Important Ideas Before Reading

Kingship

Consider what makes a ruler powerful, legitimate, or responsible.

Civilization and the Wilderness

Pay attention to how the epic contrasts city life, human culture, animals, and the natural world.

Mortality and Fame

The epic repeatedly asks how human beings should respond to death and whether achievement can create a form of lasting memory.

Questions to Keep in Mind

  • What makes Gilgamesh a good or bad king?
  • What does Enkidu gain and lose when he enters civilization?
  • Why do the gods help humans in some moments and punish them in others?
  • How does Gilgamesh’s understanding of death change?
  • What can human beings achieve despite being mortal?
