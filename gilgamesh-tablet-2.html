Tablet II: The Taming of Enkidu

Enkidu enters human society, travels to Uruk, confronts Gilgamesh, and becomes the companion who will redirect his life.

Tablet II at a Glance

Tablet II continues Enkidu’s transformation from a man of the wilderness into a participant in human society. Shamhat brings him among shepherds, where he learns to eat bread, drink beer, dress himself, and protect the community.

After hearing about Gilgamesh’s behavior in Uruk, Enkidu travels to the city and physically blocks him. Their violent struggle does not end with one man destroying the other. Instead, it produces mutual recognition and begins their friendship.

The tablet then turns toward the Cedar Forest expedition. Gilgamesh proposes confronting Humbaba and gaining lasting fame, while Enkidu, who knows the forest and its guardian, understands the danger more clearly.

Detailed Sequence of Events

1. Enkidu leaves the wilderness with Shamhat

Enkidu accepts Shamhat’s invitation to leave his former life and move toward human society. His separation from the animals has already made a return to the wilderness impossible, so his journey now moves forward rather than backward.

Shamhat acts as his guide. She does not merely take him physically toward Uruk; she introduces him gradually to the practices and expectations of human life.

2. Enkidu meets the shepherds

Enkidu arrives among shepherds, who are initially struck by his unusual appearance and strength. Although he resembles Gilgamesh in physical power, he has no experience with ordinary social customs.

This scene marks an intermediate stage between the wilderness and the city. The shepherds live in an organized human community, but they remain close to animals, pasture, and the open landscape. Enkidu can therefore learn human behavior without entering Uruk immediately.

3. Enkidu learns to eat bread and drink beer

The shepherds place bread and beer before Enkidu, but he does not initially understand how to consume them. These are processed human foods, unlike the vegetation and water that sustained him in the wilderness.

Shamhat encourages him to eat and drink. After doing so, Enkidu becomes joyful and participates more fully in the community. Bread and beer therefore function as signs of settled human life, agriculture, preparation, and shared culture.

This moment is sometimes described as the completion of Enkidu’s humanization. However, the transformation is not biological. He is learning the customs through which human beings recognize one another as members of society.

4. Enkidu is washed, clothed, and anointed

Enkidu cleans himself, receives clothing, and has oil applied to his body. These actions alter his outward appearance and make him recognizable as a civilized man.

Clothing is especially important because it separates his new life from his earlier existence among animals. His body is now presented through human customs rather than through complete natural exposure.

The process also shows that civilization is learned through repeated practices. Enkidu is not transformed by one action alone; he passes through intimacy, speech, food, drink, grooming, clothing, labor, and social responsibility.

5. Enkidu protects the shepherds

Enkidu begins guarding the shepherds and their animals. His extraordinary strength, once used to free animals from hunters, is now used to protect a human community from predators.

This is a major change in how his power functions. In the wilderness he stood against human control over animals. Among the shepherds he becomes part of a human system that raises and protects animals.

His strength has not disappeared; its social purpose has changed. This anticipates the larger role he will play in redirecting Gilgamesh’s power.

6. Enkidu learns of Gilgamesh’s conduct

A traveler or visitor tells Enkidu about a wedding in Uruk and about Gilgamesh’s claim to approach the bride before her husband.

The exact institution behind this episode is uncertain, but the narrative clearly presents Enkidu as outraged by Gilgamesh’s abuse of royal power. He decides to go to Uruk and prevent him from acting.

Enkidu’s entry into society therefore immediately becomes moral and political. He does not simply adopt civilization’s customs; he also judges the behavior of its ruler.

7. Enkidu enters Uruk

When Enkidu arrives, the people gather around him because his strength and appearance remind them of Gilgamesh. He seems to be the equal that the city has lacked.

The people’s reaction confirms the purpose of his creation. They recognize that someone has finally appeared who may be capable of confronting their king.

Enkidu’s entrance also fulfills the meaning of Gilgamesh’s dreams from Tablet I. The powerful companion represented by the falling objects has now physically arrived.

8. Enkidu blocks Gilgamesh

Enkidu positions himself in Gilgamesh’s path and refuses to allow him to pass. Their confrontation becomes a wrestling match that shakes the doorway and surrounding structures.

This is the first moment in which Gilgamesh encounters someone who can physically resist him. Until now, his strength has allowed him to act without meaningful opposition.

Enkidu therefore provides the limit Gilgamesh has been missing. He does not challenge him through superior rank or divine command, but through equality.

9. The struggle ends in recognition

The contest does not lead to lasting hatred. After the struggle, Enkidu acknowledges Gilgamesh’s exceptional status, while Gilgamesh recognizes Enkidu as a worthy equal.

Their rivalry becomes friendship because each finds in the other something he has lacked. Gilgamesh gains an equal companion; Enkidu gains a place, a relationship, and a role within the human world.

Physical conflict therefore becomes a form of recognition. Their strength no longer needs to be directed against one another and can instead be combined.

10. Gilgamesh proposes the Cedar Forest expedition

Gilgamesh turns quickly from friendship toward heroic ambition. He proposes travelling to the Cedar Forest and confronting Humbaba, its terrifying guardian.

Gilgamesh understands that human life is limited and argues that a glorious achievement can preserve a person’s name after death. His desire for fame is therefore already connected to his awareness of mortality.

At this stage, however, Gilgamesh treats mortality mainly as a reason to seek dangerous achievement. He has not yet experienced the grief that will later make death personally terrifying.

11. Enkidu warns Gilgamesh about Humbaba

Enkidu knows the Cedar Forest and understands the danger posed by Humbaba. He describes the guardian’s frightening power and attempts to discourage the expedition.

This creates an important contrast between the two friends. Gilgamesh possesses ambition but lacks direct knowledge of the danger. Enkidu possesses knowledge but is more cautious.

Their relationship will repeatedly operate through this tension: each man encourages, restrains, or influences the other, though not always wisely.

Difficult Ideas Explained

Why are bread and beer so important?

Bread and beer represent agriculture and food transformed through human labor. Eating and drinking them marks Enkidu’s participation in settled culture rather than his dependence on what grows or flows naturally in the wilderness.

Why does Enkidu protect domesticated animals?

His role has changed. He previously protected wild animals from human traps, but he now protects a human community and the animals under its care. His strength has been incorporated into social life.

Why does Enkidu become angry about the wedding?

The episode shows that he has already developed a sense of social justice. He recognizes that Gilgamesh is using royal power to violate the lives and relationships of his people.

Who wins the wrestling match?

The surviving text and different reconstructions can make the exact physical outcome difficult to describe with certainty. More important than identifying a simple winner is the fact that Enkidu successfully confronts Gilgamesh and earns his respect.

Why do they become friends immediately after fighting?

The struggle allows each man to recognize the other as an equal. Neither has previously possessed a relationship based on comparable strength and mutual respect.

Why does Gilgamesh want to fight Humbaba?

He wants to prove his courage, perform an extraordinary deed, and establish a name that will survive his death. The expedition is motivated by both heroic aspiration and dangerous pride.

Major Themes in Tablet II

Civilization as a learned process

Enkidu enters civilization through food, clothing, grooming, language, work, morality, and relationships. No single act completes his transformation.

The redirection of strength

Enkidu’s physical power remains constant, but its purpose changes. He moves from disrupting hunters to protecting shepherds and finally to restraining Gilgamesh.

Equality as the basis of friendship

Gilgamesh and Enkidu form a bond because neither is simply subordinate to the other. Their friendship begins with recognition between equals.

Fame as an answer to mortality

Gilgamesh already understands that he will die, but he initially responds by seeking a heroic reputation. He hopes that memory can compensate for the impossibility of physical immortality.

Knowledge versus ambition

Enkidu knows how dangerous Humbaba is, while Gilgamesh is driven by the glory of the proposed deed. The tablet contrasts experience with heroic desire.

Why Tablet II Matters

Tablet II completes Enkidu’s movement into human society and brings him into direct contact with Gilgamesh. The equal created to restrain the king becomes his closest companion instead.

The tablet also begins the epic’s first major heroic expedition. From this point onward, Gilgamesh and Enkidu act as a pair, and their combined strength creates both extraordinary achievements and severe consequences.

Questions to Consider

  • Which specific customs make Enkidu recognizable as a member of human society?
  • Has Enkidu been “tamed,” or has he simply learned to direct his strength differently?
  • Why does Enkidu defend the values of civilization so quickly after entering it?
  • Does the wrestling match change Gilgamesh’s behavior, or only give him a companion?
  • Is the Cedar Forest journey motivated more by courage, fear of death, pride, or desire for fame?
  • Which man shows better judgment about Humbaba at this point?
